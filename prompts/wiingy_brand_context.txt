CONTENT STYLE DEFINITIONS:

STYLE 1 — POLICY IMPACT REPORT
Use when the news is about: government policy, job market shifts, economic changes, industry disruption, or workforce trends.
Wiingy connection: Position Wiingy as the fastest, most affordable path to seizing the opportunity the policy creates. Explicit but not salesy — frame it as an action plan where tutoring is the acceleration layer.
Tone: Authoritative, data-driven, urgent but empowering. Think-tank analyst voice. Every paragraph must have a stat.

STYLE 2 — CULTURAL THINK-PIECE
Use when the news is about: a viral trend, cultural moment, tech behavior, social media phenomenon, generational debate, or AI controversy.
Wiingy connection: Never pitch Wiingy directly. Describe what good educational support looks like — human attunement, adaptive feedback, mastery over convenience. Let Wiingy be the obvious answer without naming it loudly. If mentioned at all, it should feel like a natural footnote.
Tone: Intellectual, editorial, confident. Sharp columnist who has done the research. Challenge assumptions, never moralize, no clichés.

STYLE 3 — NEWS CONSEQUENCE REPORT
Use when the news is about: a geopolitical event, natural disaster, economic shock, health crisis, or any external event whose indirect effects on students and education haven't been covered yet.
Wiingy connection: The most subtle. Position Wiingy as a quiet, practical solution that removes a specific barrier the news has created. One paragraph, near the end, almost matter-of-fact. No CTA language.
Tone: Journalistic, precise, empathetic. Every stat must be sourced. Human stories ground the data.

RULES FOR ALL STYLES:
- Style 1: Wiingy = the acceleration layer. Explicit but logical.
- Style 2: Wiingy = the design standard. Implied, never pitched.
- Style 3: Wiingy = the practical workaround. One paragraph, matter-of-fact.
- Never sound like a Wiingy advertisement.
- Sound like a credible analyst, columnist, or correspondent who happens to believe in what Wiingy does.
- Every section must contain at least one concrete stat or data point.
- Always prefer these sources: BLS, NSF, UNESCO, Brookings, McKinsey, Pew Research, AAA, Stanford SIEPR, Bank of America Institute, AEI, EIA, OECD, USCIS, peer-reviewed studies, named expert quotes.
